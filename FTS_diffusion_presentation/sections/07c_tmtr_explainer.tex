\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
\begin{column}{0.66\textwidth}\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}\setlength\topsep{0pt}
  \item Total training length is constant (S\&P 500: $5$y). Only the \emph{composition} varies.
  \item Sweep: synthetic proportion $p \in \{0\%,10\%,\dots,100\%\}$.
  \item Score: MAPE on the real held-out test set, averaged over $20$ seeds.
  \item \textbf{Difference vs TATR:} TATR grows the training set with synthetic. TMTR keeps total size fixed and \alert{replaces} a fraction with synthetic.
\end{itemize}
\end{column}

\vspace{1mm}
\begin{alertblock}{What TMTR asks}
At what synthetic proportion does the LSTM stop matching the real-only baseline? A flat curve $\Rightarrow$ the synthetic is at least as good as real. A rising curve $\Rightarrow$ the synthetic data hurts.
\end{alertblock}
\note{TMTR is the second downstream benchmark we run alongside TATR. The composition of the LSTM training set varies (real-only, mixed, synthetic-only) while its total length stays fixed. The score is the held-out MAPE averaged over 20 seeds. The diagnostic value is identifying the breakdown proportion where the synthetic data stops being equivalent to real.}
